    Abstract¶

    In this study, the authors developed a fundamental theory of interval timing behaviour, inspired by the learning-to-time (LeT) model and the scalar expectancy theory (SET) model, and based on quantitative analyses of such timing behaviour. Our experiments used the peak-interval procedure with rats. The proposed model of timing behaviour comprises clocks, a regulator, a mixer, a response, and memory. Using our model, we calculated the basic clock speeds indicated by the subjects behaviour under such peak procedures. In this model, the scalar property can be defined as a kind of transposition, which can then be measured quantitatively. The Akaike information criterion (AIC) values indicated that the current model fit the data slightly better than did the SET model. Our model may therefore provide a useful addition to SET for the analysis of timing behaviour.

    Introduction¶

    What is time? Among the innumerable, difficult problems as yet unsolved by humans, this may be one of the toughest. The ultimate goal of the first author is the mathematical axiomatization of psychological time (subjective time) achieved through an analysis founded on behavioural science. Physicists have long expressed objective time as a real number line. Subsequently, in the last century, objective time was embodied in Minkowski spacetime, a four-dimensional space possessing time as the fourth coordinate, by theorists of relativity. It seems to us, however, that even Zenos paradoxes, such as Achilles and the tortoise and the arrow, are as yet unsettled (Vlastos (1967), Alper and Bridger (1997)). Some of the important open questions that remain are the following: What is subjective time like?; How do organisms perceive time?; and How are organisms perceptions of time reflected in their behaviour? We must collect facts about the characteristics of subjective time before we can identify its underlying mathematical axioms. The present analysis targets identifying such axioms.

    Time perception and interval timing¶

    Perceived time is also called psychological time, and it is distinct from physically measured time. Unlike most perceptions, which are based on the senses, the perception of time is not associated with specific organs or external stimuli that create an organisms experiences of time. Nonetheless, not only multicellular organisms but also single-celled organisms, such as paramecia and the true slime mould Physarum polycephalum, have time perception (Saigusa et al. 2008).

    Interval timing is based on time perception. The mechanisms of circadian timing and millisecond timing have been clarified, to some extent. Interval timing, however, differs from circadian timing and millisecond timing in its precision. Interval timing is less precise than either of the other two types of timing: it is accurate only to within a wide seconds-to-minutes-to-hours range, while circadian timing is precise to within a narrow range of around twenty-four hours, and millisecond timing is of a mixed precision that lies within the subsecond range (Buhusi and Meck 2005). Given this difference in precision, it is likely that the mechanism underlying interval timing differs from those underlying these other two forms of timing.

    In the current study, fixed interval (FI) schedules of reinforcement were used to study interval timing. With FI schedules, only the first response that occurs after a fixed duration is reinforced. To better study the full course of responding over time, Roberts (1981) invented the peak-interval (PI) procedure. The PI procedure includes both FI trials and probe trials. In probe trials no reinforcement is provided, and the length of the trial greatly exceeds the length of the intervals used during the FI trials. Interval timing is often investigated through use of the PI procedure (See Fig.9 for actual data from our experiment, shown as dots; also see Fig. 2 in Church (1984); Zentall (2006)).Footnote 1

    SET and LeT¶

    Investigations of vertebrates sensitivity to time have culminated in many theories. Among them, the scalar expectancy theory (SET) model and the learning-to-time (LeT) model are mathematically interesting.Footnote 2

    Gibbon (1977) and his colleagues developed the SET model of timing (Gibbon 1977; Gibbon et al. 1984). SET is very successful at describing subjective time across many interval lengths. In short, SET provides a cognitive and stochastic account of information processing to explain temporal behaviour in animals. The components of SET are a pacemaker, a switch, an accumulator, memories, and a comparator, and the model proposes that together these components produce instrumental responses (Fig.1. See also Fig.1 in Allman et al. (2014) and its detailed neurological review). The explicit solution of a PI procedure is

    \[ R(t)= a\exp \left\{ -\frac{\,1\,}{\,2\,}\left(\frac{\,t-t_{0}\,}{\,b\,}\right)^{2} \right\}+R_{0}, \]

    (1)

    where R(t) is the operant response function at time t, t 0 is the t-coordinate of the vertex of the graph of R(t), b is the standard deviation, and a and R 0 are parameters. This is a modified version of the density function for the normal distribution.

In later trials, previous delay lengths stored in the reference memory are compared with the number in the accumulator, and the ratio of the two numbers determines the subsequent behaviour

    Machado (1997) developed the LeT model of timing, retaining the basic elements of the behavioural theory-of-timing (BeT) model, which was developed by Killeen and Fetterman (1988); Machado (1997); Machado and Guilhardi (2000); Killeen and Taylor (2000).Footnote 3 In short, the LeT model is a behavioural and deterministic account of learning formulated to explain temporal behaviour in animals. Although this theory is similar to LeT in several ways, we hope that, like SET, it will be successful at describing subjective timing behaviour across a wide range of interval lengths.

    A new model of timing behaviour¶

    Since mathematics is a deductive system, it is convenient to construct a deterministic model of timing behaviour. However, this does not imply that a useful stochastic model of this timing behaviour could not be developed.

    Model of clocks; functions X n (t)¶

    Gibbon et al. (1984) incorporated into SET the assumption that an internal pacemaker exists. Machado used clocks modelled by a cascade (stream flow) as behavioural states in the LeT model (Machado 1997). Our model also assumes the existence of clocks, although we do not yet know how these clocks are related to the organisms counting-like behaviours (Meck (1996, 2006); Fetterman et al. (1998); Matell and Meck (1999); Staddon and Higa (1999); Grondin (2001); Matell et al. (2003); Clment and Droit Volet (2006); Saigusa et al. (2008); Beckmann and Young (2009)). We are thus willing to consider models that offer precise accounts of specific types of timing behaviour as well as those that provide more general accounts of a wider range of timing behaviours.

    The cascade model used to create our model of timing behaviour is illustrated in Fig.3. This cascade model is simple, and it has much in common with the cascade model that is part of the LeT model. In our model, the containers are aligned vertically, and each container is analogous to a clock. These clocks each provide information to the organism about how much of the interval has elapsed, and the volume with which a given clock provides this information is equivalent to the amount of water in the corresponding container.

    Model of mixer; function R s (t)¶

    We use R s (t) to denote the psychometric function, the source of the operant response, which is produced by the mixer, in the sth session of a trial.

    In our model, we define R s (t) such that

    \[ R_{s}(t)=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{m}c_{n}(s)X_{n}(t), \]

    (7)

    a linear combination of functions X n (t) with coefficients c n (s) (n = 0, 1, 2, , m). For a typical example in which \(\frac {\,1\,}{\,\lambda \,}=15\) seconds, the length of the trial is 90 seconds, and m = 3, this becomes

    \[ \begin{array}{@{}rcl@{}} R_{s}(t) &=&\sum\limits_{n=0}^{3}c_{n}(s)X_{n}(t) \\ &=&c_{0}(s)X_{0}(t) +c_{1}(s)X_{1}(t) \\ &&+c_{2}(s)X_{2}(t) +c_{3}(s)X_{3}(t). \end{array} \]

    (8)

    Please note that c n (s) is not limited to positive real numbers. Let us go one step further into metaphor. If c n (s) > 0, then at a given time t, the living organism hears its internal nth clock claiming, Its time (the interval has elapsed), X n (t), amplified by the rate c n (s), taken as an activating motive. If c n (s) < 0, then at a given time t the living organism hears its internal nth clock claiming, Its not time, X n (t), amplified by the rate |c n (s)|(=c n (s) > 0), taken as an inhibitory motive; and, if c n (s)=0, then the living organism does not hear the nth clock. The term mixer is used to designate the component of the model involved in this process, which leads to an automatic summation of c n (s)X n (t) (n = 0, 1, 2, , m).

    For any natural number m, the functions X n (t) (n = 0, 1, 2, , m) are linearly independent, which can be shown mathematically. Therefore, for a given R s (t), their linear combination in Eq. (7) is unique. At the same time, Eq. (7) can be read as a decomposition of R s (t) into independent components X n (t) (n = 0, 1, 2, , m). Thus, in this study, both our total model and our method of analysis are a Poisson decomposition (PD).Footnote 4

    In the LeT model (Machado 1997), R(t) is defined as

    \[ \begin{array}{@{}rcl@{}} R(t) &=&A\sum\limits_{n\geqq 1}W_{n}(t)X_{n}(t) \\ &=&\sum\limits_{n\geqq 1}\left\{AW_{n}(t)\right\}X_{n}(t), \end{array} \]

    where W n (t) is the associative strength function of state n (n = 1, 2, 3, ) at time t, and A is a positive constant. Since \(AW_{n}(t)\geqq 0\) for all n and t, the series R(t) contains no negative term. Thus, the LeT model cannot include inhibitive processes that reduce the likelihood that a subject will respond as if a particular interval has elapsed. With X n (t) in our PD model, the notion of clocks is changed from the behavioural states in the LeT model and permits the use of negative terms.

    Fit by Pearsons correlation coefficient and AIC¶

    The Akaike information criterion (AIC) is defined to be

    \[ \begin{array}{@{}rcl@{}} \text{AIC}&=&-2\ln(\text{maximum likelihood})\\ &&+2(\text{number of adjusted parameters}). \end{array} \]

    This criterion indicates which of two models best describes a given data set, based on both how well it accounts for the variation in the data and on how few free parameters it has; if a model has a smaller AIC, then it is considered to be the better model based on these two criteria (Akaike 1974).Footnote 6

To this end, our standard setting was m = 3, which incorporated into the model the assumption that the rats had used the clocks X 0(t), X 1(t), X 2(t), and X 3(t).     Discussion¶

    Since Yi (2007) has already compared the SET and the LeT models in terms of goodness of fit and complexity, and has concluded that both models do equally well, we will not further consider the LeT model.

    Model of clocks; functions X n (t)¶

    Generally speaking, it is possible that \(\frac {\,d\,}{\,dt\,}X_{0}(t)\) is not a proportional function of X 0(t). It may be another function, such as \(\frac {\,d\,}{\,dt\,}X_{0}(t)=-\lambda \left \{X_{0}(t)\right \}^{\alpha }\) ( > 0 and 1; for example, \(\alpha =\frac {\,1\,}{\,2\,}\) by Bernoulli), \(\frac {\,d\,}{\,dt\,}X_{0}(t)=-\lambda \ln \left \{X_{0}(t)+1\right \}\), or other possibilities. Moreover, \(\frac {\,d\,}{\,dt\,}X_{0}(t)\) may depend not only on the amount of water in the container used in the model but also on the size and shape of the hole, as well as on the shape of the container. In fact, there exist many options. Nevertheless, we assumed simply that \(\frac {\,d\,}{\,dt\,}X_{0}(t)\) depends only on the amount of water X 0(t), and that \(\frac {d}{\,dt\,}X_{0}(t)\) is proportional to X 0(t). Additionally, we assumed simply that \(\frac {d}{\,dt\,}X_{n}(t)\) depends only on X n 1(t)X n (t) and that \(\frac {d}{\,dt\,}X_{n}(t)\) is proportional to X n 1(t)X n (t) (for any n = 1, 2, 3, ) with the same proportional coefficient . The reasons for our choice are as follows:

    1. (i)

      The set of Eqs. (3) and (5) is simple.

    2. (ii)

      The solutions of the differential equations are gamma density functions whose peak times are equidistant.

    We note that gamma density functions are also the foundation of the behavioural theory-of-timing (BeT) stochastic counter model.

    Model of regulator; functions c n (s)X n (t)¶

    We assumed that the c n (s) are constant even within sessions. Examining the dynamical fit for the c n (s) on s is an appropriate and important project for future studies of learning.

    In the case of c n (s)=0, the living organism hears nothing from the nth clock. If it does not use the clock X n (t), then we can reduce the AIC value of PD by up to 2 for each n.

    If we let

    \[ R_{s}(t)=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{m}c_{n}(s)X_{n}(t)+R_{0}, \]

    using constant R 0, then R 0 should be 0. The reason is as follows: in the trial interval 0 t t 1, if t 1 + and t +, then R s (t) 0; i.e., if a probe trial lasts too long, then the rat will no longer respond at all by the end of the trial.

    Model of operant response; function \(\underline {\overline {R_{s}(t)}}\)¶

    In our model, the equation \(\underline {\overline {R_{s}(t)}}=R_{s}(t)\) holds when 0 R s (t) 1, but the function \(\underline {\overline {R_{s}(t)}}\) could be defined as another function, depending on the hardware, such as an animals motor system.

    Basic speed of clocks¶

    In a PI-30 schedule, for example, if \(\frac {1}{\,\lambda \,}=15\) (s), then we may say, roughly, that a rat times the interval of 30 s by using the 15 s and 45 s clocks for inhibition, in addition to the 30 s main clock.

    Coefficients by Poisson decomposition¶

    In Sections 2.2 and 5.2, we noted that the weights c n (s) are assumed to be constant within a session. From Table 2, moreover, we may assume that the values of c 0(s), c 1(s), c 2(s), and c 3(s) are stable against changes in the FI length, and that they are almost constant in each rat from the 21st to the 70th sessions. This implies that the rats cope with the change in the FI length primarily by changing the basic period of their clocks. The weights of c 0(s), c 1(s), c 2(s), and c 3(s) express the organisms proper relative use of clocks X 0(t), X 1(t), X 2(t), and X 3(t), and the weights are almost fixed, which implies a transposition.

    Generally, in light of the mathematical fact noted in Section 2.4, the scalar property is present. Thus, the scalar property can be considered to be a transposition.

    Fit by Pearsons correlation coefficient and AIC¶

    With c 0(s), c 1(s), c 2(s), and c 3(s) fixed for each rat to the values presented in Table 2, we again calculated the fits for PD. Let PD\((\overrightarrow {c\,})\) represent the process used to fit this revised version of the PD model to the data. In PD\((\overrightarrow {c\,})\), since there is only one free parameter , its AIC value is reduced by the reduction in the number of free parameters.     Obtaining the weights c n (s) using PD, we first observed that they were approximately constant. Even with this assumption which was only approximately met by the data, PD\((\overrightarrow {c\,})\) fit the data far better, as judged by AIC values, than did SET. Therefore, we propose that PD and PD\((\overrightarrow {c\,})\) may be useful additions to SET in the analysis of timing behaviour.

    Total model¶

    Additions to SET in the preceding section may sound strange, because this paper might have appeared to be a rival for SET. Indeed, asymmetrically shaped graphs, such as Fig.4 in Cheng and Meck (2007), Fig.1 in Oprisan and Buhusi (2013), Fig.1 in McAuley et al. (2006) and others, turned out to be good applications for the PD model. Symmetrically shaped graphs, such as Fig.2 in Church (1984), Fig.3 in Church et al. (1991), Figs. 2 and 3 in Allman et al. (2014), Fig.4 in Oprisan and Buhusi (2013) and others, are similar, but are also still good applications for the SET model.

    It is inevitable that a theme of our future research will be to apply our model to various other timing schedules, such as simultaneous temporal processing (for example, see Fig.11 in Church (1984)). Also we regard it as an important theme for future research to check whether our model is compatible and cooperative to with the striatal beat-frequency (SBF) model, as in Oprisan and Buhusi (2013; also see Fig.4 in Oprisan and Buhusi 2013).
